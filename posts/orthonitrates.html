Orthonitrates

Orthonitrate ions or \(\mathrm{NO_{4}^{3-}}\), are ions consisting of one nitrogen atom and 4 oxygen atoms.
Together they form an anion in an tetrahedral arrangment. This means it got a center atom that holds onto 4 similar atoms of a different gas element. An example of a relatively stable anion is \(\mathrm{SO_{4}^{2-}}\), also known as the sulphate/sulfate ion, in contrast to orthonitrate, that is very unstable.

Already in 1977 was it proven to have existed [1], after it has been mistaken for an orthonitrite (\(\mathrm{NO_{3}}\)).

Per now only two compunds exist that has that ion: sodium orthonitrate (\(\mathrm{Na_{3}NO_{4}}\)) and potassium orthonitrate (\(\mathrm{K_{3}NO_{4}}\)). Sodium orthonitrate specifically was very reactive to moisture and carbon dioxide in the air following up to these reactions [1]:
$$\mathrm{Na_{3}NO_{4} (s) + H_{2}O (g) \rightarrow NaNO_{3} (s) + 2NaOH (s)}$$ $$\mathrm{Na_{3}NO_{4} (s) + CO_{2} (g) \rightarrow NaNO_{3} (s) + Na_{2}CO_{3} (s)}$$
